1. INTRODUCTION
1.1. Purpose of the Evaluation
The objective of this study is to conduct a Summative Evaluation of the Kinetic Grid High-Fidelity (Hi-fi) prototype. Following the iterative design process, this phase aims to validate whether the digital solution successfully addresses the usability gaps identified in the low-fidelity (Lo-fi) stage. The evaluation focuses on measuring operational efficiency, the effectiveness of error prevention mechanisms, and overall user satisfaction across a diverse demographic of facility operators.
1.2. Scope of Evaluation
The evaluation encompasses a comprehensive assessment of the end-to-end management workflow, testing the following five core modules:
* Intelligent Dashboard: High-level status monitoring and activity tracking.
* Integrated Connectivity: Hardware discovery and batch pairing via Radar.
* Facility Overview: Multi-criteria court filtering and searching.
* Granular Court Details: Real-time session control and safety management.
* Strategic Scheduler: Resource allocation using a Gantt-style interface.

2. STUDY DESIGN AND EVALUATION PLANNING
2.1. Participant Profiles (Grouped by Background)
To ensure statistical diversity and evaluate the system across different mental models, six participants were recruited and categorized into three distinct groups:
* Group 1: Power Users (High Technical Proficiency / High Domain Knowledge)
   * Phát (20): Part-time sports center staff. Extremely tech-savvy; prioritizes rapid execution and high-efficiency shortcuts.
   * Anh Uy (35): F&B Manager and sports enthusiast. Expert in complex POS systems; highly critical of workflow logic and system throughput.
* Group 2: Moderate Users (Tech-Literate / Low Domain Knowledge)
   * Mẹ Hân (48): Housewife assisting in management. Background in accounting; proficient in Excel and basic social media (Zalo, YouTube). Values data precision over aesthetics.
   * Chị Uy (21): Student. Proficient in general technology but has zero knowledge of sports rules or terminology. Relies strictly on visual affordances and iconography.
* Group 3: Novice Users (Low Technical Proficiency / Low Domain Knowledge)
   * Mợ Út (50): Traditional court owner. Transitioning from manual paper-based tracking; hesitant and fearful of "breaking" digital systems.
   * Má Uy (52): Housewife. Uses only basic communication apps; no interest in sports. Requires a simplified, self-explanatory UI with minimal cognitive load.

	2.3. Research Methodology & Measures
The study utilizes a Within-Subjects Design, where every participant interacts with all system modules to provide a comparative analysis of cross-demographic behavior.
Quantitative Measures:
* Time on Task (ToT): Duration in seconds from task initiation to completion.
* Error Rate: Frequency of "slips" (accidental clicks) or "mistakes" (incorrect mental models).
* Task Completion Rate: Percentage of tasks completed (Independent vs. Assisted).
Qualitative Measures:
* Thinking Aloud Protocol: Real-time verbalization of thoughts and frustrations during interaction.
* Post-test Debriefing: Semi-structured interviews to capture emotional responses and overall perceived satisfaction.
2.4. Task Scenarios
The tasks represent the core operational flow of the system, addressing issues identified during the Lo-fi phase:
* Task 1: System Initialization. "The facility has just opened. Use the app to discover and connect all 24 laser projectors simultaneously."
* Task 2: Resource Discovery. "A customer calls to ask for an available Badminton court. Use the filters to find an empty slot as quickly as possible."
* Task 3: Live Operation. "Court 2 is currently active. Access the court details to check the remaining time, adjust the laser intensity, and book a future session for this specific court."
* Task 4: Strategic Scheduling. "Customer Nguyễn Văn A wants to book a court at 19:00. Use the Scheduler to verify availability and confirm the booking."

	2.6. Evaluation Procedure
The study follows a standardized 6-step workflow:
1. Briefing: A casual introduction. Users are informed that the system is being tested, not their ability.
2. Pre-Questionnaire: Collection of demographic data and technology habits.
3. Introduction: Brief overview of Kinetic Grid. Briefing depth is adjusted by group (Novices receive more conceptual detail; Power Users receive only a high-level summary).
4. Execution: Participants perform the 4 tasks while being recorded. Facilitator prompts "Thinking Aloud."
5. Post-Questionnaire: Completion of the PUEU (Perceived Usefulness and Ease of Use) survey.
6. Debriefing: Discussion of specific pain points or "critical incidents" observed during the session.

4. DISCUSSION & DESIGN RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the detailed analysis from the testing phase, this chapter synthesizes core strengths and remaining experience barriers, proposing specific enhancements for the next iteration.
4.1. Major Successes
* Efficiency through Automation (Task 1): The "Connect All" feature represents a significant leap from the Lo-fi version. It not only allows Power Users (Phát, Anh Uy) to achieve peak efficiency but also helps Novice users (Mợ Út) overcome the psychological barrier of interacting with multiple hardware devices simultaneously.
* Safety by Design (Task 3)[i]: The application of Defensive Design principles by resizing and isolating the "Kill Switch" button has proven highly effective. This is a critical success in preventing fatal errors among users with limited manual dexterity (Má Uy).
* Recognition over Recall (Task 2): The Semantic Color-coding system demonstrated its effectiveness by enabling users unfamiliar with sports (Chị Uy) or technology (Mợ Út) to make accurate decisions based on visual intuition without needing to process excessive text.

4.2. Identified Usability Issues
* Visual Overload in Scheduling (Task 4): The information density on the Gantt chart remains too high for elderly users. The absence of text labels on color blocks made it difficult for users with low domain knowledge (Chị Uy) to interpret the data effectively.
* Accessibility & Ergonomics (Task 3): Fine-grained interaction components, such as sliders, have hitboxes that are too small, leading to "slips" for both high-speed users (Phát) and those with lower motor precision (Má Uy).
* Mental Model Mismatch (Task 1): The technical term "Radar" created ambiguity for traditional users (Má Uy), leading to delays in understanding the system initialization process.
4.3. Actionable Recommendations
To further optimize the Kinetic Grid system, the following three groups of improvements are proposed:
* Visual Enhancements:
   * Increase the font size on the Scheduler's timeline by at least 20%.
   * Incorporate sport labels (Text labels) directly onto the colored blocks in the schedule to support users with low domain knowledge.
* Interaction Optimization:
   * Expand the Hitbox for settings buttons [k]and light intensity sliders.
   * Integrate a Zoom-in feature for the Gantt chart or provide a "Simplified View" (list format) for Novice users.
* Error & Guidance Refinement:
   * Implement a two-step Confirmation Dialog for the Kill Switch to ensure absolute operational safety.
   * Update the terminology from "Radar" to "Scanning for Devices" to better align with everyday language and user mental models.

5. CONCLUSION
The Summative Evaluation of the Kinetic Grid system has provided empirical evidence of the project's operational viability. Through the behavioral analysis of six personas representing the full spectrum of target users, it was determined that the Hi-fi version is not merely a visual upgrade but a significant advancement in safety and operational efficiency.
The system achieves a sophisticated balance between "Expert Efficiency" (via filters, search, and batch actions) and "Novice Confidence" (via safe design and intuitive color-coding). While accessibility barriers persist for elderly users in high-density information views, Kinetic Grid has proven its core value: transforming complex laser infrastructure management into a simple, safe, and accessible digital experience for all facility owners. The project is ready for real-world implementation following the proposed ergonomic and display refinements.
